## Overview

This project evaluates the effectiveness of interactive simulations in improving agricultural risk management education among minority students with limited prior exposure to farming. Agricultural production involves significant uncertainty from weather, markets, and policy, yet access to hands-on risk management training remains uneven.

Using the simulation game *Augustaland Farms*, participants make production and risk management decisions in a cattle and hay operation, including the use of federally subsidized insurance and forward contracts. The analysis focuses on how simulation-based learning influences decision-making behavior and knowledge acquisition.

## Methodology

* Pre- and post-simulation surveys to measure knowledge gain
* Separate assessment of understanding across risk management tools (e.g., insurance products, forward contracting)
* Non-parametric **Wilcoxon signed-rank tests** to evaluate statistically significant changes in knowledge levels

## Tools used
* Excel - Data organization
* R - Data cleaning, analysis

## Key Findings

* Participants showed statistically significant improvements in understanding of production risk and insurance tools, based on Wilcoxon tests.
* Knowledge gains were particularly strong for insurance-related concepts (e.g., PRF insurance), where initial familiarity was low.
* Participants adopted more structured risk management strategies within the simulation, frequently utilizing federally subsidized insurance options.
* While insurance use reduced simulated net profits, it improved risk mitigation behavior and decision consistency.
* Participants reported increased confidence in managing agricultural risk after completing the simulation.

## Contribution

This project provides empirical evidence that simulation-based learning can effectively improve both knowledge and decision-making in agricultural risk management. It also offers a scalable framework for delivering targeted, inclusive education programs for underrepresented groups in agriculture.
